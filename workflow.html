Designing for resolution, not deflection

An agentic workflow design, worked end to end: "My terminal won't take payments"

Worked example for Flatpay's merchant-facing Voice AI · Prepared by Adam Grøfte Barfod · July 2026

Purpose and scope

This document specifies an agentic workflow for a merchant-facing Voice AI handling one high-stakes call type, "My terminal won't take payments", end to end: problem framing, signal composition, flow design, permissions, edge cases, escalation, localization, stress-testing, and post-launch measurement. Scope covers one call type across six markets (DK, FI, FR, DE, IT, UK). Status: a product-design level specification prepared for validation, before engineering discovery, without inside knowledge of Flatpay's systems; every dependency on internals is written as a working assumption and consolidated with its fastest validation path in the assumptions section. Intended readers: support leadership and the AI team.

1Problem framing

A merchant calls: "My terminal won't take payments." There is a queue of customers behind them. Every minute unresolved is lost revenue and visible embarrassment in front of their customers. This is the call where support quality is judged.

The sentence sounds like one problem. It is at least six, spread across different systems, and only some of them are about the terminal:

Six readings of one reported symptom
What the merchant saysWhat may actually be wrongWhere the truth lives
"It won't take payments" / "It's dead" / "Nothing happens"Local connectivity is down (Wi-Fi, SIM, router)Terminal telemetry, network heartbeat
"It keeps saying error" / "It rejects the card"Card-side declines (issuer, not merchant)Transaction log, decline codes
"It froze" / "The screen is stuck"Device fault or pending software updateDevice status, firmware version
"Payments stopped working today"Account-level restriction (a risk, KYC or settlement hold)Account status systems
"Everyone here has this problem"Platform or processor incidentIncident status, call-volume spike
"The system isn't working"Different device entirely (POS tablet, printer), or user errorProduct registry + guided questions

This is why the workflow cannot be built from FAQ content. Merchants describe symptoms, in their own words, under stress. Root causes live in systems. The design job is orchestration: composing what the merchant says with what the systems show, and routing to the fix.

Why this workflow first: a strong candidate for early validation because the problem is urgent, system-observable and potentially resolvable. Actual volume and feasibility would be confirmed from call, ticket and platform data before any build decision.

2Signal map: check before you ask

A great support agent doesn't ask "is it plugged in?" when the dashboard shows the terminal heartbeating. The AI should reach for signals first and use questions to disambiguate, not to gather what systems already know.

Signal sources and what each distinguishes
SourceSignalsWhat it distinguishes
Terminal telemetry, where terminals report status to a central systemOnline/offline, last heartbeat, battery, firmware version, error stateConnectivity vs. device fault vs. "actually fine"
Transaction logRecent attempts, outcomes, decline codes, last successful payment"No attempts arriving" vs. "attempts arriving and declined"
Account statusSettlement state, KYC/risk holds, payout blocksHardware problem vs. account problem
Incident statusOpen platform/processor incidents, real-time call-volume spike on this symptomOne merchant's problem vs. everyone's problem
Merchant profileMarket, language, product suite (terminal-only vs. POS bundle), hardware model, opening hoursWhich script, which market overlay, which device
Support historyRecent tickets and calls, what was already tried, open casesFirst contact vs. repeat contact (different path entirely)
Design rule · the repeat-caller gate

Support history is checked before diagnosis. A merchant calling the second time about the same issue must never be walked through the same script again. Repeat contact on the same symptom within 7 days routes to a human with full history attached, unless the previous contact ended in a verified resolution, which makes this a new fault. Nothing erodes trust in an AI faster than making a frustrated caller repeat a failed procedure.

3The workflow

The control flow is deterministic: predefined stages and branch logic, with the language model doing judgment work inside stages (symptom classification, natural conversation, summarization). This follows the reliability principle that production agentic systems should be workflows first, agents only where the task genuinely requires open-ended behavior. Every call carries an explicit status at all times: routed to a diagnosis branch, needs clarification (a provisional route plus a required next action, never a forced conclusion), handoff to a human specialist, or no fault confirmed.

0 · Answer & stabilizeAcknowledge urgency in the merchant's language: "Let's get you taking payments again." Set expectations for the next two minutes. No account data and no payment fallbacks yet: those wait for verification, because offering payment alternatives to an unverified caller is a card-not-present fraud channel.
▼
1 · Identify & verifyIdentify via caller ID, org number, or terminal ID; authenticate with a real second factor (one-time code or callback to the number on file) since all three identifiers are public or spoofable. Tiered assurance: hardware troubleshooting at low assurance, account data and payment fallbacks (a payment link, where the market supports one) only after authentication. Then: the repeat-caller gate.
▼
2 · Compose signalsFetch telemetry, transaction log, account status, incident status in parallel while asking one high-yield question: "What does the screen show when you try?" Signals + answer feed classification.
▼
3 · Route to a diagnosis branchLLM classifies the symptom; deterministic rules pick the branch. If confidence is low: one clarifying question, max two loops, then human.
▼
APlatform incident
Tell the merchant it is not their fault, give status and expected resolution, log for callback. Suppress troubleshooting.
BLocal connectivity
Guided recovery: network check, reboot, SIM or Wi-Fi failover where the hardware supports it. Verify each step against telemetry, not just "did that work?"
CDevice fault / software
Remote checks, push update if safe (confirm first). If hardware is dead: replacement flow with clear timeline.
DAccount-level restriction
Never speculate about reasons on the call. Acknowledge, state that a specialist must handle it, warm-transfer with priority.
ECard-side declines
Show the terminal works: point to decline codes, explain issuer declines, reassure. Evidence, not apology.
FAmbiguous / other device
Disambiguate device (terminal vs. POS tablet vs. printer). One more signal or question; if still unclear, human.
▼
4 · Act & verify with ground truthAfter any fix: confirm with ground truth. A terminal-to-host connection test, a payment attempt run by the merchant, or telemetry showing the next live transaction go through. "It should work now" is not a resolution; a working terminal, observed, is.
▼
5 · Close, or hand off warmClose: verified success, summary spoken and logged, ticket written. Hand off: warm transfer with the full context package (see Escalation and handoff) so the merchant never repeats themselves.

The canonical decision path, end to end

The flowchart answers "where did the case travel"; the executable decision model's rule trace answers "why". How to read it: rounded boxes are process steps, diamonds are decisions, cut-corner boxes are hard gates that outrank diagnosis, and slanted boxes are warm handoffs. Chips at the right are diagnosis branches reached directly from a gate; the dashed readouts are state changes, not routes. Terminology matches the decision model exactly, and a full text version follows below.

decision_path · canonical route static spec · live terminology
Text version of the flowchart
  1. Call begins, then stabilize: calm the urgency and set expectations.
  2. Hard gate, verify caller: tiered assurance before any account data. If verification fails, the case becomes needs clarification: provisional route only, account actions locked.
  3. Hard gates, human first: a requested human is honored on the first ask, and a repeat contact whose prior fix was never verified goes straight to a warm handoff.
  4. Signal availability: if the signal APIs are unreachable, the workflow enters degraded mode with symptom-only diagnosis and reduced autonomy.
  5. Hard gate, payment-availability blockers: payment acceptance restricted or blocked routes to branch D, account-level restriction, with a specialist warm handoff; this outranks any technical fault.
  6. Incident status: a confirmed incident routes to branch A, platform incident, with broadcast mode when a spike joins it; a spike alone enters suspected-incident mode while conservative diagnosis continues.
  7. Device identification: a wrong or unclear device routes to branch F; still unclear after two clarification attempts becomes a warm handoff.
  8. Technical diagnosis: the signals are composed with the reported symptom and fan out to the six diagnosis branches A to F. Without decisive evidence the case is needs clarification, carrying a provisional route and a required next action.
  9. Permission overlays: every action is classed autonomous, confirm first, locked or never.
  10. Act, then outcome verification: a verified resolution in telemetry closes the case; two failed fix cycles are a hard stop into a warm handoff with the full context package, so the merchant never repeats themselves.

4What the AI may do: permissions

Permission levels and allowed actions
LevelActions
AutonomousRead the minimum purpose-limited signals required for the active workflow, subject to caller verification, market configuration and audit logging; guide reboot and network recovery; explain decline codes; resend receipts; create and update tickets; schedule callbacks; send self-serve links.
Confirm firstPush software updates; remote-restart a terminal; order replacement hardware; change non-financial settings. State what will happen, get an explicit yes.
LockedActions blocked by verification or system state, not prohibited outright: account data and payment fallbacks while caller assurance is low; every account-linked action while verification has failed; software updates on DE fiscal devices pending TSE validation; remote actions while the signal systems are down. A locked item unlocks the moment the blocking state clears.
NeverDiscuss the reasons behind a risk or KYC hold; change payout accounts or financial settings; quote settlement amounts without verified data; promise refunds, credits or waivers; keep a merchant in troubleshooting after two failed fix cycles.
Compliance guardrails · non-negotiable in all six markets

The assistant identifies itself as AI at the start of every call, as a trust principle and in preparation for the EU AI Act transparency obligation applying from 2 August 2026. Call recording and transcript use rest on a documented lawful basis with clear notice, purpose limitation, data minimization, access controls, retention rules and data-subject rights, with legal review per market. Transcripts used for testing are redacted and de-identified; material that remains reasonably linkable to a person continues to be treated as personal data. The assistant never requests card data; recording is paused, suppressed or prevented from capturing it where possible, and accidental capture is immediately redacted under applicable PCI controls. These are design constraints, not features: any workflow change is checked against them before release.

5Edge cases: where this workflow will misbehave

Edge cases are grouped by where they break the flow. Each one becomes a permanent test case in the stress-testing suite: found once, tested forever.

Voice channel

Voice-channel edge cases and designed behavior
Edge caseDesigned behavior
Noisy shop floor, music, espresso machineASR tuned and tested against in-domain noise; if transcription confidence drops, switch to short confirm-back questions rather than guessing.
Merchant interrupts mid-sentence (barge-in)Stop speaking within ~200 ms (a provisional target, to be re-baselined on Flatpay's own audio), address the interruption, retain context. Tested explicitly, per branch.
Accents, second-language speakers, code-switching (a Danish merchant switching to English)Language detection per utterance; respond in the merchant's dominant language; never silently switch.
Merchant puts the phone down to walk to the terminal; long silencesDistinguish "working silence" from "gone": announce wait expectations, re-prompt gently at intervals, offer a callback if the line stays dead.
Caller says "the system" but means the POS tablet, the printer, or the cash drawerDevice disambiguation early in branch F, driven by the product registry: "Is it the card terminal itself, or the screen you run orders on?"
Multiple terminals on the accountIdentify which unit by asking for the terminal's screen name or last digits of the serial; never assume.
Caller is an employee, not the account ownerTroubleshooting: allowed. Account data and account changes: verification threshold not met, so offer to proceed with hardware steps only.

Diagnosis

Diagnosis edge cases and designed behavior
Edge caseDesigned behavior
Conflicting signals: telemetry says online, merchant says deadTrust neither alone, and issue no final branch: the case is needs clarification with a provisional route until a terminal-to-host connection test runs. A connectivity failure routes to branch B, a device failure to branch C, a pass moves scrutiny to the remaining evidence, and an unavailable test tool means proceeding on the symptom with reduced autonomy.
Merchant has already rebooted ("I tried that")Believe them. Skip the step, say why the next step differs. Repeating known-failed steps destroys credibility.
Two problems at once (offline and an account-level restriction)Identify all active blockers. Resolve or escalate the blocker that currently determines payment availability, and carry secondary findings in the case context. An account-level restriction can block payments even with a healthy terminal.
Intermittent fault ("it works sometimes")Do not force a binary diagnosis. Log a pattern-watch on telemetry, set expectations, schedule follow-up rather than a fake fix.
Symptom described in retrospect ("it didn't work this morning")Check the transaction log for the time window instead of live troubleshooting a device that now works.
Classification confidence low twice in a rowStop looping. Two clarification attempts maximum, then warm handoff. The merchant's patience is the budget.

Action & trust

Action and trust edge cases and designed behavior
Edge caseDesigned behavior
Fix verified failed twiceHard stop. Escalate with everything tried. Never a third loop.
Merchant demands a human immediatelyComply on the first request, gracefully, with context passed. The human path is a feature, not a failure. No retention scripts.
Anger, distress, or a threat to churn detectedSentiment triggers escalation priority even if the diagnosis is going well.
Terminal battery dead or device unusable during the callSwitch channel: steps by SMS or email, callback scheduled after a charging window.
The AI's own signal sources fail (telemetry or transaction APIs down, possibly during the very platform incident being diagnosed)Degrade honestly: fall back to question-driven diagnosis with reduced autonomy and say the systems are temporarily unavailable. If the Voice AI itself is degraded, route straight to a human with honest queue messaging. Design for missing data, not only wrong data.
Mass incident: a sudden spike of the same symptomIncident and spike are separate facts. A confirmed incident routes to branch A with normal incident handling; confirmed incident plus a spike flips branch A into broadcast mode (short queue-buster message, status page, callback list); a spike without a confirmed incident enters suspected-incident mode, where conservative diagnosis continues while the incident process is flagged.
After-hours call, human queue closedSay so honestly, take a structured callback booking, send self-serve steps in writing. Never simulate a human queue that doesn't exist.

6Escalation and handoff

Triggers

  • Deterministic: branch D (account-level restrictions); two failed fix cycles; verification failure on account actions; repeat contact within 7 days.
  • Model-signaled: low classification confidence after two loops; negative sentiment threshold; explicit human request.
  • Recorded as a list: every fired trigger is preserved in the order it fired; a later trigger never erases an earlier one.

Context package

  • Who: merchant, market, product suite, verified level.
  • What: symptom in the merchant's words + branch classification.
  • Signals: telemetry, transaction log, account flags checked.
  • Done: every step tried, with outcomes.
  • State: sentiment note and what the merchant was promised.

Transfer mechanics

  • Warm transfer with a whisper brief to the receiving agent.
  • Route by branch to the right queue (risk/KYC is not the hardware queue).
  • Manage dead air: tell the merchant what is happening.
  • Hard rule: the merchant never repeats themselves.

7Localization: one workflow, six market overlays

Denmark, Finland, France, Germany, Italy, and the UK differ in language, product suite, compliance, and self-serve surfaces. The design principle: localize behavior, not logic. One canonical workflow, with a per-market overlay that parameterizes it. Six forked workflows would drift apart within a quarter; a shared flow with overlays keeps every improvement global by default.

Market overlay dimensions
Overlay dimensionExamples of what varies
Language & ASRPer-market speech models and phrasing; per-utterance language detection; tested noise and accent sets per language.
Product suiteTerminal-only vs. full POS bundle (screen, printer, cash drawer) changes device disambiguation and fix scripts.
ComplianceFiscal rules, such as TSE requirements in Germany, constrain what a "safe fix" is: an action that breaks fiscal logging is never autonomous in that market.
Self-serve surfacesWhat the merchant can be sent a link to do themselves differs per market; branch scripts reference the local surface, never a global one.
Escalation targetsLocal queue hours, languages, and specialist coverage; after-hours behavior configured per market.

Rollout follows the same shape: prove the canonical workflow in one market, then add overlays one market at a time, with the market's support leads reviewing scripts before go-live.

8Stress-testing before a live merchant hears it

Six test layers with methods and pass criteria
LayerMethodPass criteria (targets)
1 · Branch logicDecision-table tests: every branch, every permission gate, every edge case in section 5 as an executable test case.100% of defined cases route correctly; no undefined states.
2 · Transcript replayReplay historical tickets and call transcripts through classification; compare to known root causes.Classification agreement with resolved history of at least 90%, a provisional bar to be reset from the first replay baseline, before pilot.
3 · Adversarial personasScripted difficult callers: furious, rambling, wrong-device, already-tried-everything, non-native speaker, silent.No loop past two cycles; graceful handoff in every failed run.
4 · Voice reliabilityASR word-error rate on clean and in-domain noisy audio; barge-in stop latency and context retention; end-to-end latency.WER under 5% clean, under 10% on tuned in-domain noisy audio; barge-in stop within 200 ms with context retained; latency under ~800 ms. All provisional targets from published voice-QA baselines, to be re-baselined on Flatpay's own audio.
5 · Shadow modeAI listens to live calls and proposes diagnosis and next step; humans act. Measure agreement between AI proposal and human action.Sustained agreement threshold met before any autonomous rollout.

9Measuring what matters after launch

Containment is not resolution. An AI that "handled the call" while the merchant still cannot take payments has failed twice: it failed the merchant, and it hid the failure from the team. This workflow has an unusual advantage: resolution is objectively verifiable.

One measurement principle · branch-specific verified outcomes

For hardware and connectivity fixes (branches B, C): the terminal processes a live transaction within 15 minutes of call end, observed in telemetry; the window is a provisional target, to be re-baselined on Flatpay's transaction-gap data per merchant type. Not "the caller didn't escalate." Not a survey. A payment. The other branches get their own success definitions: incident communicated and callback kept (A), case with the right specialist inside the SLA (D), merchant confirms understanding (E).

Post-launch metrics and what each protects against
MetricWhat it protects against
Verified resolution rate (per branch)Deflection dressed up as success; shows exactly which branches earn autonomy and which don't.
Escalation rate with reason codesSilent overload of human agents; reason codes rank what to build next.
Repeat contact within 7 daysFake fixes that bounce back; the single best detector of a workflow that closes too eagerly.
Handoff quality (did the merchant repeat themselves?)Warm transfer decaying into cold transfer; audited via transcript sampling.
Merchant satisfaction, AI-closed vs. human-closed callsResolution achieved at the cost of trust.

The improvement loop: a weekly review of escalations, repeat contacts, and failed verifications. Each failure is classified: missing signal, missing branch, bad script, or model error. Fixes are prioritized by calls affected times resolution lift, and each becomes a new test case in the stress-testing suite. New data-source investments (for example connecting router diagnostics, or logistics tracking for replacements, to be validated with ops and logistics) are ranked the same way. Per-branch targets should be set from Flatpay's own replay baseline rather than industry averages, which vary widely with how loosely "resolved" is defined. That definition discipline is the point of the measurement principle above.

+What I would build next, inside Flatpay

With access to real systems, data and the teams, weeks 2 to 4 of this work produce the artifacts a production rollout needs: a cross-functional service blueprint, a system integration and failure map, a full permission and verification matrix, a warm-handoff sequence design, a stage-gated rollout plan with hold and rollback outcomes, a metric tree with branch-specific verified outcomes, and a failure taxonomy with named owners.

These are deliberately not mocked up here: built on assumptions instead of Flatpay's real systems, they would be speculation wearing the costume of rigor. Knowing which artifact belongs at which moment, and building it against reality rather than guesswork, is the job.

11Design principles behind the specification

Workflows over free agents. Deterministic control flow with model judgment inside steps: reliability first, autonomy earned per branch.
Compose signals; don't recite FAQs. If a system can answer it, never ask the merchant.
Verify with ground truth. A resolution is a processed payment, not a polite goodbye.
The human path is a feature. Escalation is designed with the same care as automation, and asking for a human always works the first time.
Localize behavior, not logic. One canonical flow, market overlays on top.
Every production failure becomes a permanent test. The edge-case library only grows.

Standards and practice this design leans on

  • Anthropic, Building Effective AI Agents: the workflows-over-agents control flow at the core of section 3, with model judgment inside steps and deterministic rules between them.
  • EU AI Act, Article 50: disclosure that the caller is interacting with an AI, at the start of every call.
  • PCI SSC, Protecting Telephone-based Payment Card Data: card numbers never requested, recordings redacted if one is read aloud.
  • GDPR, Regulation (EU) 2016/679: consent, retention and transcript anonymization per market.
  • NIST AI Risk Management Framework and ISO/IEC 42001: the staged shadow, assisted, autonomous rollout, human oversight and per-branch measurement are aligned with these frameworks (aligned with, not certified against).
  • DORA, Regulation (EU) 2022/2554: the honest degraded mode and incident handling are consistent with its operational-resilience themes for payment firms.
  • KassenSichV (DE fiscal device rules, TSE): why software changes to fiscal devices in the DE overlay are never autonomous pending validation.
  • Contact-center practice (COPC CX Standard, ICMI): first-contact resolution and repeat-contact rate as quality metrics, and the containment vs. resolution distinction in section 9. Practitioner guidance, not a formal standard.
  • Published voice-agent QA benchmarks: source of the provisional WER, barge-in and latency targets in section 8, to be re-baselined on Flatpay audio.
